Photometric Estimates

This site was designed to produce accurate estimates of the photometric zeropoint between a set of observations (instrumental magnitudes) and the standard photometric systems of SDSS, USNOB1, and 2-MASS. To do this, I pull observations from the SDSS catalog (if within the SDSS footprint) or from the all-sky USNO-B1 catalog, cross-match them to sources in the all-sky 2-MASS catalog, and then fit a model spectra from the Pickles Stellar Atlas to each source. This produces an estimate for the spectrum for each individual source, from which I then produce synthetic photometry for all requested bands. These photometric estimates can then be used to calculate a systematic offset between observed magnitudes (e.g. the instrumental magnitudes generated through photometry) and the true magnitudes for a set of observations; i.e. the photometric zeropoint.

Characteristic Errors

Though the estimates for individual sources can sometimes be off by as much as .5 mag, I have worked to get the mean offset for any ensemble of sources as close to zero as possible.
When using SDSS (e.g. to predict y-band within the SDSS footprint), characteristic errors of individual sources are of order .10 mag, and errors of the mean offset for all sources in field are of order .05 mag.

Histograms of the differences between predicted and true values for SDSS magnitudes for sources in 5 arbitrary SDSS fields, as estimated by the other SDSS observations and 2-MASS. Each histogram displays sources within a .25° box around the listed RA and DEC.


When using USNO-B1 (e.g. to predict the SDSS magnitudes outside the SDSS footprint), characteristic errors of individual sources are of order .20 mag, and errors of the mean offset for all sources in field are of order .05 mag.

Histograms of the differences between predicted and true values for SDSS magnitudes for sources in 5 arbitrary SDSS fields, as estimated by the USNO-B1 and 2-MASS observations. Each histogram displays sources within a .25° box around the listed RA and DEC.


Note: estimates for the bluer bands (u, g, r) produced from USNO-B1 values very near the galactic plane are systematically worse than those presented above - approaching .2 mag (for the mean offset) within 5° of the plane. This is probably due to galactic reddening by interstellar dust, but I am, as yet, unable to get rid of the problem. For now, I'd recommend against using u, g, or r estimates by the PhotoZP Estimator closer than 10° away from the galactic plane.
